Step 1: Define the Question

The most important step. A vague question leads to wasted effort and useless answers.

Clarify the Business Problem

Before touching any data, understand what decision your analysis will inform:

  • ‚Ä¢ What decision needs to be made? (launch a product, change pricing, hire more staff)
  • ‚Ä¢ Who is the audience? (CEO needs summary, team needs details)
  • ‚Ä¢ What would success look like? (what answer format is useful)
  • ‚Ä¢ When is it needed? (quick answer vs. deep dive)
Turn Vague Questions into Specific Ones
‚ùå Vague
  • "How are we doing?"
  • "Why are sales down?"
  • "Is the campaign working?"
  • "What should we do?"
‚úì Specific
  • "What was Q3 revenue vs Q2?"
  • "Which regions declined? By how much?"
  • "Did conversion rate increase after launch?"
  • "Which 3 products have highest margins?"
Questions to Ask Stakeholders
  • ‚Ä¢ "What will you do differently based on the answer?"
  • ‚Ä¢ "What's your hypothesis? What do you expect to see?"
  • ‚Ä¢ "What time period should I look at?"
  • ‚Ä¢ "Are there specific segments you care about?"
  • ‚Ä¢ "Have you looked at this before? What did you find?"
  • ‚Ä¢ "What does [term] mean in this context?"

Step 2: Collect and Understand the Data

Identify Data Sources
  • ‚Ä¢ What data exists? Databases, spreadsheets, APIs, third-party tools
  • ‚Ä¢ Where does it come from? User actions, system logs, manual entry
  • ‚Ä¢ How fresh is it? Real-time, daily, weekly updates
  • ‚Ä¢ Who owns it? Who to ask about definitions and access
Understand the Data Structure

Step 3: Clean and Validate

Never trust data blindly. Always validate before analyzing.

Data Quality Checklist

Check for:

  • ‚òê Missing values (NULLs, blanks)
  • ‚òê Duplicates
  • ‚òê Outliers (unusually high/low)
  • ‚òê Invalid values (future dates, negative prices)
  • ‚òê Inconsistent formats (date formats, case)
  • ‚òê Data type issues (numbers as text)

Validation queries:

-- NULL check
SELECT COUNT(*) 
WHERE column IS NULL

-- Duplicate check
SELECT id, COUNT(*)
GROUP BY id HAVING COUNT(*) > 1

-- Value distribution
SELECT status, COUNT(*)
GROUP BY status
Sanity Checks

Compare your data against known facts:

  • ‚Ä¢ Does total revenue match finance reports?
  • ‚Ä¢ Does customer count match CRM?
  • ‚Ä¢ Are there orders on days the business was closed?
  • ‚Ä¢ Do averages seem reasonable for your industry?

Step 4: Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Before answering specific questions, understand your data's shape and patterns.

Univariate Analysis

Examine one variable at a time:

  • ‚Ä¢ Distribution: Histogram, box plot
  • ‚Ä¢ Central tendency: Mean, median, mode
  • ‚Ä¢ Spread: Range, std dev, IQR
  • ‚Ä¢ Categories: Frequency counts
SELECT
    AVG(sales),
    MIN(sales),
    MAX(sales),
    COUNT(*)
FROM orders
Bivariate Analysis

Examine relationships between variables:

  • ‚Ä¢ Correlation: Do they move together?
  • ‚Ä¢ Comparison: How do groups differ?
  • ‚Ä¢ Trends: How does Y change with X?
-- Compare by category
SELECT
    region,
    AVG(sales),
    COUNT(*)
FROM orders
GROUP BY region
Time Series Analysis

For data over time, look for:

Trend

Long-term direction (up, down, flat)

Seasonality

Repeating patterns (weekly, monthly, yearly)

Anomalies

Unusual spikes or drops

Changes

Points where pattern shifts

Segmentation

Break down by meaningful categories:

-- Segment by customer type, region, product, channel, etc.
SELECT
    customer_segment,
    region,
    COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id) AS customers,
    SUM(revenue) AS total_revenue,
    AVG(revenue) AS avg_order
FROM orders
GROUP BY customer_segment, region
ORDER BY total_revenue DESC

Pareto Analysis (80/20 Rule)

Find what drives most of the impact:

-- Which products drive 80% of revenue?
SELECT
    product_name,
    revenue,
    SUM(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY revenue DESC) AS cumulative,
    SUM(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY revenue DESC) / 
        SUM(revenue) OVER () AS cumulative_pct
FROM product_sales
ORDER BY revenue DESC
Root Cause Analysis (5 Whys)

Drill down to find the real cause:

1. Why are sales down? ‚Üí Fewer orders
2. Why fewer orders? ‚Üí Fewer website visitors
3. Why fewer visitors? ‚Üí Less paid traffic
4. Why less paid traffic? ‚Üí Budget was cut
5. Why was budget cut? ‚Üí ‚Üê Root cause!

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Confirmation Bias

Looking for data that supports what you already believe. Always try to disprove your hypothesis.

Correlation ‚â† Causation

Two things changing together doesn't mean one causes the other. Ice cream sales and drownings both rise in summer.

Simpson's Paradox

A trend can reverse when you segment the data. Always check if patterns hold within subgroups.

Cherry-Picking

Selecting only time periods or segments that support your story. Show the full picture.

Sample Size Issues

"Conversion doubled from 1 to 2 users" isn't meaningful. Consider statistical significance.

Averages Hiding Information

Average of 100 and 0 is 50, same as two 50s. Look at distributions, not just averages.

🧭 Interface Basics

Tableau has one core mechanic: drag a field onto a shelf. Everything else is variation. Learn what each shelf does and what the blue/green colour coding means, and the rest of the tool stops feeling arbitrary.

🔵🟢 Dimensions vs Measures

The most important concept in Tableau, and it's colour-coded so you never have to guess. Every field is one or the other.

Dimensions — blue

Categorical. Answers what or who.

  • Region, Category, Customer Name
  • Dates (usable either way)
  • IDs, codes, labels

They slice the data — adding one creates more rows or columns.

Measures — green

Numeric and aggregatable. Answers how much.

  • Sales, Profit, Quantity, Discount
  • Automatically summed or averaged

They fill the data — adding one gives every slice a number.

The one-sentence rule

Dimensions slice; measures fill. "Sales by Region" is one measure sliced by one dimension. Nearly every chart you will ever build is some arrangement of that sentence.

Watch out for numeric IDs

Tableau sees any numeric column as a measure, including things like Postal Code and Row ID. Summing postcodes is meaningless. Drag those fields up into the Dimensions area to fix it once, permanently.

🔀 Discrete vs Continuous — the second colour rule

This trips up nearly everyone, because it's a separate distinction from dimension/measure. Blue means discrete; green means continuous.

Discrete — distinct values

  • Creates headers — separate labelled panes
  • Finite, separable values
  • Can be sorted and grouped

Continuous — an unbroken range

  • Creates an axis — a continuous scale
  • Any value in between is meaningful
  • Used for lines and positions

The same field, two colours, two charts

Both of these are MONTH(Order Date) over the same four years of data. The only difference is which one you picked from the right-click menu.

MONTH blue · discrete
Twelve buckets. All four years pooled into each month. Answers: is there seasonality?
MONTH green · continuous
One timeline running straight through. Answers: are we growing?

Right-click any date pill and you get two lists of the same words. The top list is blue and discrete; the bottom is green and continuous. Picking from the wrong one is why a line chart sometimes comes out as disconnected chunks.

Where it bites: dates

Right-click a date pill and you get two lists. Picking Year from the top (blue, discrete) gives you separate columns per year — good for comparing 2016 against 2017. Picking Year from the bottom (green, continuous) gives you one unbroken time axis — good for showing a trend. Same field, same word, completely different chart. When a line chart mysteriously splits into disconnected chunks, this is why.

📋 Data pane (left)

Every field in your source, dimensions above measures. Drag from here onto shelves or straight onto the canvas.

📐 Columns & Rows

Your X and Y axes. Columns = horizontal, Rows = vertical — the naming confuses everyone at first.

🎨 Marks card

How the data points look. Colour, size, labels, tooltips. Where charts become expressive.

🔍 Filters & Pages

Filters limit what's shown. Pages steps through values one at a time — useful for animating over time.

🖼️ Canvas

The view itself. You can drop fields directly here and Tableau guesses where they belong.

📊 Show Me (top right)

Suggests chart types for the fields you've selected, greying out the ones that won't work.

Detail is the one worth understanding early

Every other Marks target changes appearance. Detail changes granularity — it splits the data into more marks without colouring or resizing anything. That's how a scatter plot goes from one dot to one dot per sub-category. If a chart shows a single mark when you expected many, you need something on Detail.

🎨 Mark types

The dropdown at the top of the Marks card. Automatic picks based on your fields and is right most of the time — but knowing the manual options unlocks charts Show Me won't suggest.

Bar

Comparison. The default for a dimension plus a measure.

Line

Trends. Automatic when a continuous date is present.

Circle / Shape

Scatter plots and dot plots.

Square

Highlight tables and heatmaps.

Area

Volume over time, stacked composition.

Text

Crosstabs and KPI numbers.

Map / Polygon

Filled geographic regions.

Pie

Unlocks an Angle target. Use sparingly.

Gantt Bar

Durations — the basis of timeline charts.

🏗️ Building your first view

  1. Drag Category to Rows

    Three row headers appear — Furniture, Office Supplies, Technology. No numbers yet, because you've sliced without filling.

  2. Drag Sales to Columns

    Tableau aggregates to SUM(Sales) automatically and draws horizontal bars. You have a chart.

  3. Sort it

    Hover the Sales axis and click the descending sort icon. Now the biggest category is on top and the chart answers its own question.

  4. Add a second dimension to Color

    Drag Region onto Color. The bars split into stacked segments — one chart now carries two dimensions.

  5. Swap the axes

    Press Ctrl W to flip rows and columns. Vertical columns instead of horizontal bars — same data, different emphasis.

The fastest way to learn: double-click everything

Double-clicking a field drops it where Tableau thinks it belongs. Double-click Sales, then Category, then Region, and watch what happens at each step. You'll build an intuition for the shelves far quicker by watching Tableau's guesses than by planning each drag.

🧠 Key intuitions

  • Dimensions slice, measures fill — the sentence that explains most charts
  • Blue is discrete, green is continuous — headers vs axes
  • Rows = Y, Columns = X — counterintuitive, worth memorising
  • Detail adds granularity without appearance
  • Dates come in blue and green versions — the cause of most surprised line charts
  • Double-click to explore — watching Tableau's guesses teaches the shelves
  • Right-click everything — nearly every option lives in a context menu
  • Numeric IDs aren't measures — drag them into Dimensions

🎯 Practice exercises

  1. Build a bar chart of Sales by Region and sort it descending
  2. Drag Segment to Color and describe what changed
  3. Press Ctrl W to swap the axes
  4. Drag Sales to Label so values print on the bars
  5. Put Category on Rows and Region on Columns — what kind of view is that?
  6. Drag Order Date to Columns and note that Tableau defaults to YEAR
  7. Right-click the date pill and switch between the blue Month and the green Month; explain the difference in one sentence
  8. Double-click Profit on an empty sheet and see what Tableau builds unprompted
  9. Build a scatter of Sales vs Profit, then drag Sub-Category to Detail — watch one dot become seventeen
  10. Click through Show Me with Category and Sales selected; note which options are greyed out and why
  11. Find Postal Code in Measures and move it to Dimensions
  12. Clear the sheet and rebuild Sales by Region from memory, without looking back
🏆 Challenge: one dataset, six views, ten minutes

Give yourself ten minutes and build six different views of Superstore — no planning, no perfectionism. Then look at them together and pick the one that tells you something you didn't already know. Speed of iteration is Tableau's actual advantage, and it's a skill you build by deliberately working faster than feels comfortable.

🖥️ The Tableau window, named

Almost every instruction in every lesson is "drag this to there" — so the first thing worth knowing is what the regions are called. Open a worksheet and this is what you're looking at.

If you only memorise three words

Field — a column in your data, listed in the Data pane. Pill — what a field becomes once you drag it somewhere. Shelf — the strip you drag it onto. Nearly every instruction in every lesson is some combination of those three.

🗃️ Your data: fields and types

Field
One column of your data. Tableau lists every field in the Data pane down the left.
Example: Superstore has 21 fields — Order Date, Region, Category, Sales, Profit and so on.
Data pane
The list of every field in your data source, down the left of the window. Dimensions on top, measures below a dividing line.
Example: you drag fields from here onto shelves to build a chart.
Pill
The coloured capsule a field becomes once you drag it onto a shelf. Same field, different place.
Example: drag Sales to Rows and you get a green SUM(Sales) pill.
Dimension blue
A field you group by. Categories, names, dates, IDs — things you can't meaningfully add up.
Example: Region, Category, Customer Name. Putting Region on Rows creates four rows.
Listed above the dividing line in the Data pane.
Measure green
A number you can add up or average. Tableau aggregates these automatically.
Example: Sales, Profit, Quantity, Discount. Drop Sales in and it becomes SUM(Sales).
Listed below the dividing line in the Data pane.
Discrete blue
Separate, countable values. On a shelf, a discrete field creates headers — one labelled slot per value.
Example: blue MONTH gives you 12 labelled columns, Jan to Dec, with all years pooled.
Continuous green
An unbroken range where in-between values are meaningful. Creates an axis, not headers.
Example: green MONTH gives one continuous 48-month timeline from 2014 to 2017.
Aggregate / aggregation
Rolling many rows up into one number — summing, averaging, counting. Tableau does this automatically for measures.
Example: Superstore has 9,994 rows. Put Region on Rows and Tableau aggregates them into 4 numbers.
Granularity (level of detail)
How finely the data is broken up — what one mark or row represents.
Example: one Superstore row = one product within one order. That's the granularity. Region on Rows makes the view's granularity "one mark per region".
Data source
The connection to your data, plus any joins, renames and type changes you made on the Data Source page.
Bottom-left tab labelled Data Source.
Extract (.hyper)
A compressed snapshot of your data saved locally, instead of querying the original every time. Faster, but needs refreshing.
Example: Tableau Public requires an extract — that's why publishing converts your connection.
Live connection
The opposite: Tableau queries the source on every interaction, so you always see current data.
Fan-out (duplication)
When joining two tables at different granularity multiplies rows, inflating totals.
Example: joining one monthly Target row to 2,000 order rows repeats the target 2,000 times, so SUM(Target) comes out 2,000× too big.

🏗️ Building a view

Shelf
A strip you drag pills onto. Each one does a different job.
Columns = horizontal axis · Rows = vertical axis · Filters = what's included · Pages = step through values one at a time.
Card
A small panel of controls. The Marks card is one; legends, filter controls and parameter controls are others.
Marks card sits under the Data pane; the rest stack on the right.
Mark
One drawn thing in your chart — a bar, a dot, a line segment, a square, a map shape.
Example: a bar chart of Sales by Region has four marks, one per region.
Mark type
What shape those marks take. The dropdown at the top of the Marks card.
Bar, Line, Circle, Square, Area, Text, Map, Pie, Gantt Bar. Automatic lets Tableau choose.
View / viz / worksheet
Three words for the same thing: one chart on one sheet. "Viz" is short for visualisation.
Detail
A Marks card target that splits the data further without changing appearance. It's how you add granularity.
Example: a Sales vs Profit scatter shows one dot until you put Sub-Category on Detail — then it shows 17.
Show Me
A panel of chart-type suggestions. Select some fields and it highlights which charts they'll support.
Button at the top right of the toolbar.
Text table / crosstab
A chart made of numbers rather than shapes — a spreadsheet-style grid. Mark type Text.
Example: Region down the side, Category across the top, Sales in the cells.
Highlight table
A text table where the cells are also shaded by value — Tableau's version of conditional formatting.
Example: Region × Category with Sales on Color, so the strongest combinations are darkest.
Small multiples
The same chart repeated once per category, side by side on a shared scale.
Example: four little sales trends, one per region, instead of four tangled lines on one chart.
Dual axis
Two measures overlaid in the same space with two separate axes, so they can use different mark types.
Example: Sales as bars and Profit Margin as a line over the top.
Header
The labels around the chart — the row and column names. Distinct from the pane.
Example: "Central", "East", "South", "West" down the left of a bar chart.
Pane
The data area of the chart — where the marks actually live. Formatting menus split settings into Header and Pane, and picking the wrong one is a common confusion.
Measure Names / Measure Values
Two fields Tableau generates so you can show many measures at once. Names is a dimension holding the measure names; Values holds all their numbers.
Example: the only way to build a table with Sales, Profit and Quantity as three columns.

🗂️ Organising and filtering data

Group
Several values of a dimension merged into one. A fixed, hand-picked list.
Example: combining East and West into a "Coastal" group.
Set
A dimension split into In and Out, often by a rule, so it updates itself as data changes.
Example: "Top 20 Customers by Sales" — put it on Color to compare those 20 against everyone else.
Bin
A measure sliced into equal-width ranges, turning it into a dimension. This is how histograms are made.
Example: Sales binned into $100 buckets, then counted — showing most orders are small.
Hierarchy
Dimensions nested so you can drill down with a + on the pill.
Example: Category → Sub-Category → Product Name, built by dragging one field onto another.
Parameter
A single value the viewer can change — a number, date or word — which calculations can then read.
Example: a "Target Margin" slider that moves a reference line on your chart.
Context filter
A filter promoted to run before the others, creating a temporary subset everything else works from.
Example: needed for "Top 10 products in the East" — otherwise Tableau finds the national top 10, then keeps only the East ones.
Right-click a filter pill → Add to Context. It turns grey.

🧮 Calculations

Calculated field
A new field you define with a formula. It joins the Data pane and behaves like any other field.
Example: SUM([Profit]) / SUM([Sales]) as a Profit Margin field.
Analysis → Create Calculated Field
Row-level calculation
Runs once per row of the original data, before any aggregation.
Example: [Profit] / [Sales] computes a margin for each of the 9,994 line items.
Aggregate calculation
Runs on already-totalled numbers. For ratios this is nearly always what you want.
Example: SUM([Profit])/SUM([Sales]) gives West a 14.9% margin; the row-level version gives 4.2%. Same data, different question.
COUNTD
Count Distinct — how many different values there are, not how many rows.
Example: COUNT([Order ID]) = 9,994 line items. COUNTD([Order ID]) = 5,009 actual orders.
Table calculation
A calculation that runs on the numbers already displayed in your view, not on the source data.
Example: running total, rank, percent of total, year-over-year growth.
Right-click a measure pill → Quick Table Calculation. A small △ appears on the pill.
Compute Using / addressing
The direction a table calculation travels through your table — across the rows, down the columns, or along a dimension you name.
Example: "% of Total" computed across makes each row sum to 100%; computed down makes each column sum to 100%.
Partition
The group a table calculation restarts within.
Example: a running total that resets each January is partitioned by Year.
LOD expression
"Level of Detail" — a calculation that aggregates at a different granularity than the view it sits in. Written in curly braces.
Example: {FIXED [Customer Name] : SUM([Sales])} totals sales per customer regardless of what's on screen.
FIXED / INCLUDE / EXCLUDE
The three LOD keywords. FIXED ignores the view; INCLUDE goes finer than the view; EXCLUDE goes coarser.
Example: FIXED at customer level lets you average customer totals inside a regional chart.

📊 Statistics you'll meet in the Analytics pane

Median
The middle value when everything is sorted. Unlike the average, a few huge values don't drag it.
Example: Superstore's average sale is about $230 but the median is about $54 — a handful of enormous orders pull the mean up. The median describes a typical order far better.
Skewed
A distribution with a long tail on one side, so mean and median disagree.
Example: Superstore sales are strongly right-skewed — most orders are small, a few are thousands of dollars.
Quartile / IQR
Quartiles cut sorted data into four equal groups. The IQR (interquartile range) is the middle 50% — the box in a box plot.
Example: whiskers default to 1.5 × IQR; anything beyond is drawn as an individual outlier.
Standard deviation
A measure of spread. Roughly two-thirds of values sit within one standard deviation of the mean, if the data is symmetric.
Skewed data breaks that assumption — prefer percentile bands there.
R² (r-squared)
How much of the variation a trend line explains, from 0 to 1. Higher means the line tracks the points more closely.
Example: R² = 0.85 means the model accounts for 85% of the variation; 0.1 means it barely explains anything.
p-value
How likely a pattern this strong could show up by chance if there were really no relationship. Smaller = less likely to be a fluke.
Example: below 0.05 is the usual threshold for "probably not chance" — but with 9,994 rows even trivial effects clear it, so check the slope too.
Slope
How much the y value changes per unit of x — the size of the effect, as opposed to whether it exists.
Example: "each extra 10% of discount costs about 4 points of margin" is a slope, and it's what a manager can act on.
Exponential smoothing
The forecasting method Tableau uses: a weighted average of history where recent periods count for more, optionally with trend and seasonality.
Example: it needs about two full years of monthly data before it can detect a seasonal pattern at all.
Prediction interval
The shaded band around a forecast showing the range the real value is likely to fall in.
Never show a forecast line without it — the band is the honest part.
RMSE / MAE / MAPE
Forecast error scores — roughly, how far off the model was on past data. Lower is better; they're only meaningful compared between models on the same data.
Shown in Forecast → Describe Forecast.
k-means clustering
An algorithm that sorts marks into k groups so members of each group are as similar as possible on the measures you give it.
Example: clustering sub-categories on Sales and Profit finds natural groups like "high volume, thin margin".
It will find groups in random data too — always sanity-check they make business sense.
Correlation vs causation
Two things moving together doesn't prove one causes the other.
Example: discount and profit move strongly against each other in Superstore — but that could be discounting causing losses, or unprofitable products getting discounted more. The chart can't tell you which.

🖼️ Dashboards and sharing

Dashboard
Several worksheets arranged on one screen, usually with interactivity between them.
Action
A rule that makes interacting with one sheet affect the others.
Example: a Filter action — clicking the West bar filters every other chart to West. A Highlight action dims the rest instead of removing them.
Tiled vs floating
Tiled objects snap into a grid and resize together. Floating objects sit anywhere, layered on top.
Tiled is the default and the right choice for almost everything.
Container
An invisible box that arranges what's inside it — Horizontal puts things side by side, Vertical stacks them.
Example: four KPI tiles in one horizontal container stay evenly sized as the dashboard resizes.
Viz in Tooltip
A whole chart shown inside a hover tooltip, filtered automatically to whatever you're pointing at.
Example: hover a region on a map and a small monthly trend for that region pops up.
KPI
Key Performance Indicator — one headline number someone tracks.
Example: total revenue, profit margin, order count, shown big at the top of a dashboard.
Tableau Public
The free version. Fully featured, but workbooks save publicly and it can't connect to databases.
Never put confidential data in it.

🖱️ Reading the instructions

Lessons use a few conventions for describing what to do. Here's exactly what each means.

Analysis → Totals → Show Grand Totals
A menu route. Click "Analysis" in the menu bar at the very top, then "Totals" in the menu that opens, then "Show Grand Totals".
"Right-click the pill → Sort"
Right-click (Ctrl-click on a Mac with one button) directly on the coloured capsule in the shelf, then choose Sort from the menu that appears.
"Drag Region to Rows"
Click and hold the field's name in the Data pane on the left, drag it onto the strip labelled "Rows", and release.
"Put Sales on Color"
Drag the field onto the small button labelled Color inside the Marks card.
Show Me
A control labelled exactly that in the application. When a term appears in this style, look for those literal words on screen.
"Double-click Measure Values"
Double-click the field's name in the Data pane. Tableau places it wherever it thinks it belongs, which is often faster than dragging.

🔬 The Analytics Pane

The second tab beside your Data pane, which most people never open. Everything in it is drag-and-drop, and all of it answers the same question: is this number actually unusual? A bar chart says West sold $725K. A reference line says that's above average. Only one of those is a finding.

📖 Terms on this page

Defined before they're used. Every term on the site is also in the glossary.

Reference line
A line drawn across the view at a value — an average, a target, a constant.
Example: An average line showing two regions above it and two below.
📍 Analytics pane → drag onto the view
Scope
Whether an Analytics item applies to the whole table, each pane, or each cell.
Example: "Compared to the company average" is Table; "compared to its own category" is Pane.
Distribution band
Shaded bands computed from the data's own spread.
Example: ±2 standard deviations, or the 25th–75th percentile.
IQR
Interquartile range — the middle 50% of values. The box in a box plot.
Example: Whiskers default to 1.5 × IQR; anything beyond is drawn as an outlier.
R²
How much variation a trend line explains, 0 to 1. Higher means it fits more closely.
Example: 0.85 means the model accounts for 85% of the variation.
p-value
How likely a pattern this strong could appear by chance if there were no real relationship.
Example: Below 0.05 is the usual threshold — but with 9,994 rows even trivial effects clear it.
Prediction interval
The shaded band around a forecast showing where the real value is likely to land.
Example: Never show a forecast line without it.
k-means clustering
An algorithm sorting marks into k groups whose members are most alike.
Example: Clustering sub-categories on Sales and Profit finds "high volume, thin margin" groups.

📈 What's in the pane

Click Analytics at the top of the left sidebar. Three groups, roughly in order of how much statistics is involved.

Summarize

Constant Line · Average Line · Median with Quartiles · Box Plot · Totals · Distribution Band · Trend Line

Descriptive. Nothing is being predicted.

Model

Average with 95% CI · Median with 95% CI · Trend Line · Forecast · Cluster

Inferential. These fit a model and can be wrong.

Custom

Reference Line · Reference Band · Distribution Band · Box Plot

The same objects, but you configure them from scratch.

Items grey out when the view can't support them — a trend line needs a continuous axis, a forecast needs a date. If something is unavailable, the view's structure is the reason, not a missing setting.

🎯 Scope — the drop zone decides everything

When you drag an item onto the view, Tableau offers three drop zones. Choosing the wrong one is the single most common reason a reference line looks wrong, and it's easy to do without noticing.

Table

One line across the whole view. The average of everything.

Pane

One line per pane — per region, per year. Each compares against its own group.

Cell

One per individual cell. Rarely useful — an average of one value is that value.

Which one do you want?

Ask "compared to what?" Comparing every sub-category against the company average is Table. Comparing each sub-category against its own category's average is Pane. They're different questions and both are legitimate — but the chart doesn't say which you chose, so decide deliberately.

You can change it afterwards: right-click the line → Edit → the Scope radio buttons at the top of the dialog.

🎚️ Reference bands and distribution bands

Reference band

A shaded region between two values you specify — each independently a constant, an aggregate, or a parameter.

  • An acceptable operating range
  • Budget floor to stretch target
  • Last year's minimum to maximum, as context for this year

Distribution band

Bands computed from the data's own spread. Choose the basis:

  • Percentages — e.g. 60%/80% of the average
  • Percentiles — 25th/75th, the middle half
  • Quantiles — split into N equal groups, shaded
  • Standard deviation — ±1, ±2 from the mean
Standard deviation bands make outliers self-evident

Add a distribution band at ±2 standard deviations, scoped to the table. Anything outside it is genuinely unusual rather than just large — and the chart now shows why you think so, which is much more persuasive than asserting it in a caption.

Standard deviation assumes a roughly symmetric distribution. Superstore's sales are strongly right-skewed, so a "−2 SD" band lands below zero and means nothing. On skewed data prefer percentile bands, which make no distributional assumption at all.

📦 Box plots

Drag Box Plot from the Analytics pane onto a view that has individual marks — a strip of points per category. It adds the five-number summary on top of them.

Whisker options
1.5 × IQR (default)
Maximum extent of the data

With 1.5×IQR, points beyond the whiskers are drawn individually as outliers.

Box plots answer a question bar charts can't

A bar chart of average order value by region hides everything about spread. Two regions with identical averages can have completely different distributions — one steady, one driven by a handful of enormous orders. The box plot shows that immediately, and it's usually the more actionable fact.

📉 Trend lines

Drag Trend Line onto a scatter plot or time series. Tableau fits a model, draws it, and — if you ask — tells you whether it means anything.

Model types

  • Linear — a straight line. The default, and right most of the time
  • Logarithmic — fast change that flattens out
  • Exponential — compounding growth
  • Polynomial — curves, degree 2 to 8
  • Power — proportional relationships

Options worth knowing

  • Show confidence bands — the uncertainty around the fit
  • Force y-intercept to zero — only when zero-in-means-zero-out is genuinely true
  • Allow a trend line per colour — separate fits per category, which often reveals that one group behaves differently
  • Show recalculated line — refits live when you select marks
Excluding a point changes the model

Right-click a mark → Exclude and the trend refits without it. That's legitimate when you can justify the exclusion — a known data error, a one-off event — and it's data manipulation when you can't. If you exclude anything, say so on the chart.

📖 Reading the model — R², p-value, and honesty

Hover the trend line for a summary, or right-click → Describe Trend Model for the full output: the formula, coefficients, R² and p-value.

Three ways a trend line misleads
  • Correlation isn't causation. Superstore shows discount and profit moving strongly against each other. That's real and worth acting on — but the chart can't tell you whether discounting causes the losses or whether already-unprofitable products get discounted more. Say "these move together", then investigate.
  • High-degree polynomials fit noise. A degree-8 curve will hug your points beautifully and predict nothing. A high R² bought by adding curves is a warning sign, not a result. Stay linear without a substantive reason.
  • Extrapolation is not supported by the line. The model describes the range you have data for. Extending it past that is an assumption you're making, not something the chart established.

A statistically significant relationship can still be practically irrelevant. With 9,994 rows, tiny effects clear p < 0.05 easily. Always report the slope alongside the p-value — "significant" says the effect probably exists; the slope says whether it's worth anyone's time.

🔮 Forecasting

Drag Forecast onto a time series. Tableau extends it using exponential smoothing, automatically detecting trend and seasonality from the history.

  1. Build a real time series first

    A continuous date on Columns and a measure on Rows. Forecast greys out without a date dimension.

  2. Drag Forecast on

    The projection appears with a shaded prediction interval. The forecast portion is drawn in a lighter shade so it's visually distinct from actuals.

  3. Open Forecast Options

    Right-click → Forecast → Forecast Options. Set the forecast length, the source data aggregation, and the model — Automatic, Automatic without seasonality, or Custom, where you set trend and season to None, Additive or Multiplicative yourself.

  4. Read Describe Forecast

    Right-click → Forecast → Describe Forecast. The Summary tab states the model chosen and whether seasonality was detected; the Models tab gives quality metrics — RMSE, MAE, MAPE — and the smoothing coefficients.

  5. Always show the prediction interval

    In Forecast Options, keep Show prediction intervals on, at 95%. The band is the honest part of the chart — it shows how wrong the estimate could be.

A forecast line on its own implies a precision the model doesn't have. Always leave Show prediction intervals switched on.

What this forecast can and cannot do

It extrapolates the pattern in your history and nothing else. It has no knowledge of a price change, a competitor launch, or a pandemic. It needs enough history to work with — at least two full cycles before it can detect seasonality at all, so two years of monthly data is the practical minimum.

Present it as "if nothing changes, roughly this, within this range" — never as a plan or a target.

Choosing k

Leave it on Automatic first — Tableau picks using a variance-ratio criterion. Then try 3, 4 and 5 by hand and see which produces groups you can name. If you can't name them, they aren't useful.

Describe Clusters

Right-click the Clusters field → Describe Clusters. The Summary tab gives each cluster's centres and size; the Models tab gives an analysis of variance showing which variables actually drove the split.

The algorithm produces “Cluster 1, 2, 3”. You supply the names — and the names are what makes it usable.

Save it, then name it

Drag the Clusters pill from the Marks card into the Data pane and it becomes a permanent group you can use anywhere — on Color, in filters, in calculations. Then rename the members from "Cluster 1" to something meaningful: "Volume drivers", "Margin drivers", "Problem products".

An unnamed cluster is a curiosity. A named one is a segmentation the business can act on.

k-means will happily find clusters in random data — it partitions whatever you give it. Before presenting them, check the groups make business sense and that the variance analysis shows a real separation. "The algorithm said so" is not a finding.

⚠️ Totals on ratios

A margin column's grand total should be total profit ÷ total sales, not the sum or the average of the individual margins. Whether Tableau gets it right depends on how the calculation was written:

  • SUM([Profit])/SUM([Sales]) — an aggregate expression, recomputed at the total level. Correct.
  • AVG([Profit]/[Sales]) — a row-level ratio, averaged. Wrong everywhere, including the total.

Leave Total Using on Automatic for a properly-written aggregate ratio — that's what makes it recompute rather than add up. Always sanity-check the total against a number you already trust.

🧠 Key intuitions

  • A number without context isn't a finding — a reference line supplies it in one drag
  • The drop zone is the analysis — Table, Pane or Cell answers "compared to what?"
  • Median on skewed data, not mean — Superstore is very skewed
  • Fill Above / Below reads faster than a line by itself
  • Percentile bands beat standard deviation when the data isn't symmetric
  • Box plots show what bar charts hide — spread, not just the average
  • Report the slope, not just the p-value — significant isn't the same as important
  • Stay linear unless you have a reason; high-degree polynomials fit noise
  • Always show the prediction interval on a forecast
  • Name your clusters or they aren't a segmentation
  • Check ratio totals — they must recompute, not add up

🏆 Challenge: find the discount cliff and put a number on it

Build a scatter of Discount against Profit Ratio by Sub-Category with a linear trend line. Find the discount level where the fitted line crosses zero — that's the point beyond which the average order loses money. Add a constant reference line there and shade the loss region.

Then answer the question a manager would ask next: how much profit would have been recovered last year if discounts had been capped at that level? Report the R² and slope so they know how much to trust it, and state plainly that the relationship is correlational. A chart, a number, a recommendation, and an honest caveat — that's a complete piece of analysis, and any one of them alone isn't.

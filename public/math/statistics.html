Statistics for Data Analysis

Why Statistics Matters

Statistics helps you make confident decisions from data. As a data analyst, you'll use statistics daily to summarize data, compare groups, detect patterns, and determine if your findings are real or just random noise.

This section covers: The practical statistics you'll actually use on the job - not academic theory, but tools for real analysis. Every concept includes real examples and exercises using sample datasets.

Sample Datasets Used in This Section:

  • ‚Ä¢ E-commerce Orders: 10,000 orders with revenue, quantity, customer info
  • ‚Ä¢ Website Analytics: Daily visitors, conversions, bounce rates
  • ‚Ä¢ Employee Data: Salaries, departments, tenure, performance
  • ‚Ä¢ A/B Test Results: Control vs treatment conversion data

üöÄ The Analyst's Real-World Workflow

In the real world, nobody hands you a dataset and says "calculate the standard deviation." They ask vague questions. Here is how you actually use these tools:

2

If you make a change to fix it, use A/B Testing to prove it worked.

1. Descriptive Statistics

Summarize and describe your data. This is where every analysis starts. Before building models or running tests, you need to understand what your data looks like.

The "center" of your data - what's typical or expected.

Sum of all values divided by count

(10 + 20 + 30 + 40 + 50) / 5 = 30

‚úì Best for: Symmetric data without extreme values

‚úì Best for: Categorical data

Less common for continuous data

A Z-score tells you how many standard deviations a value is from the mean.

Z = (Value - Mean) / Std Dev

Example: Is a $150 order unusual?

Z = (150 - 100) / 25 = 2.0

2 std devs above mean ‚Üí top ~2.5%

Interpreting Z-scores:

  • |Z| < 1: Common (68% of data)
  • |Z| 1-2: Somewhat unusual
  • |Z| 2-3: Unusual (~5% of data)
  • |Z| > 3: Very rare, possible outlier

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Correlation Does NOT Mean Causation

This is the single most important concept in statistics. Just because two things are correlated doesn't mean one causes the other.

Spurious Correlation

"Ice cream sales" correlates with "Drowning deaths"

Both caused by a third factor: Hot weather

Confounding Variable

"Shoe size" correlates with "Reading ability" in children

Both caused by age - older kids have bigger feet AND read better

Reverse Causation

"Hospital visits" correlates with "Deaths"

Sick people go to hospitals, not hospitals making people sick

How to establish causation:

  • ‚Ä¢ Randomized controlled experiment (A/B test) - Gold standard
  • ‚Ä¢ Natural experiment - External event creates random groups
  • ‚Ä¢ Instrumental variables - Advanced econometric technique
  • ‚Ä¢ Time ordering + theory - Cause must precede effect, plus logical explanation

The most important practical skill for data analysts. Determine if observed differences are real or just random chance.

What is A/B Testing?

Compare two versions of something to see which performs better, using randomization to establish causation.

The Question: Is the 0.3 percentage point difference REAL, or just random chance?

The Logic of Hypothesis Testing

We can't prove B is better. Instead, we try to rule out that the difference is due to chance.

Step 1: State the Hypotheses

H‚ÇÄ (Null): "No real difference"

The default assumption we try to disprove

H‚ÇÅ (Alternative): "There IS a difference"

What we're trying to prove

Step 2: Calculate the p-value

"If there were truly no difference, what's the probability of seeing results this extreme by random chance?"

Small p-value = unlikely to be chance = evidence against H‚ÇÄ

üí° Understanding p-values (This is crucial!)

What p-value IS:

"Assuming the null hypothesis is true (no real difference), the probability of observing a result at least as extreme as what we got."

Example: p = 0.03

"If A and B were truly identical, there's only a 3% chance we'd see a difference this large by random luck."

Since 3% is pretty unlikely, we conclude the difference is probably real.

What p-value is NOT:

  • ‚ùå NOT "the probability B is better than A"
  • ‚ùå NOT "the probability the null hypothesis is true"
  • ‚ùå NOT a measure of how BIG the effect is

How big is the gap?

2. The Noise (Standard Error)

How uncertain are we? (Simplistic approximation: 1/‚àöN)

1/‚àö30,000 ‚âà 0.6% (very rough)

The Rule of Thumb:

If Signal < 2 √ó Noise, it's probably just luck.

üßÆ How to Calculate Significance (Z-Test) from Scratch

Going beyond the napkin math. This is how we get the exact p-value.

1. The Inputs

Control (A)

Users ($n_1$): 10,000

Conv ($x_1$): 320

Rate ($p_1$): 0.032

Treatment (B)

Users ($n_2$): 10,000

Conv ($x_2$): 350

Rate ($p_2$): 0.035

2. Pooled Probability ($\hat{p}$)

If H‚ÇÄ were true, both groups are the same. What's the overall rate?

$\hat{p} = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{n_1 + n_2} = \frac{320 + 350}{20,000} = \frac{670}{20,000} = 0.0335$

3. Standard Error (SE)

How much do we expect the difference to vary by chance?

$SE = \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2})}$

$SE = \sqrt{0.0335(0.9665)(\frac{2}{10,000})} = \sqrt{0.0323(0.0002)} \approx 0.00254$

4. Significance Calculation

How many standard errors apart are they?

$Z = \frac{p_2 - p_1}{SE} = \frac{0.035 - 0.032}{0.00254} = \frac{0.003}{0.00254} = 1.18$

Lookup Z=1.18: p-value ‚âà 0.238 (or 23.8%)

Not Significant (p > 0.05)

‚ö†Ô∏è Common A/B Testing Mistakes

1. Peeking at Results

Checking p-values repeatedly and stopping when you see significance inflates false positive rate.

‚úì Fix: Decide sample size upfront, don't stop early

2. Underpowered Tests

Not enough users to detect the effect size you care about.

‚úì Fix: Calculate required sample size before starting

3. Multiple Testing Without Correction

Testing 20 metrics? One will be "significant" by chance alone.

‚úì Fix: Pre-specify primary metric, use Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons

4. Ignoring Practical Significance

A 0.01% lift can be "statistically significant" with enough data, but meaningless for business.

‚úì Fix: Ask "Is this effect size worth acting on?"

üßÆ How to Calculate a Confidence Interval from Scratch

Want to put a range on your estimate? Here is the recipe.

1. Get Sample Stats

  • Sample Mean ($\bar{x}$) = $100
  • Std Dev ($s$) = $20
  • Sample Size ($n$) = 64

2. Calculate Standard Error (SE)

The uncertainty of the mean decreases as square root of n.

$SE = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{20}{\sqrt{64}} = \frac{20}{8} = 2.5$

3. Choose Confidence Level (Z)

How sure do you want to be?

  • 90% Confidence ‚Üí Z = 1.645
  • 95% Confidence ‚Üí Z = 1.96 (Standard)
  • 99% Confidence ‚Üí Z = 2.576

4. Margin of Error ($ME$)

$ME = Z \times SE = 1.96 \times 2.5 = 4.9$

5. The Result

Interval = [Mean - ME, Mean + ME]

Interval = [100 - 4.9, 100 + 4.9]

[ $95.10, $104.90 ]

6. Outlier Detection

Find unusual values that may be errors, fraud, or genuinely interesting cases.

Methods for Detecting Outliers

Z-Score Method

How many std devs from the mean?

Z = (value - mean) / std_dev

Common threshold: |Z| > 3

‚úì Good for: Normal distributions

‚úó Affected by outliers themselves

IQR Method (Tukey's Fences)

Based on quartiles - robust to skew

Lower fence: Q1 - 1.5 √ó IQR

Upper fence: Q3 + 1.5 √ó IQR

Outside fences = outlier

‚úì Good for: Skewed data, robust to extremes

‚úì Used in box plots

What To Do With Outliers

Keep them if:

  • ‚Ä¢ It's a valid data point (big customer, holiday spike)
  • ‚Ä¢ It represents important edge cases
  • ‚Ä¢ You're analyzing the full distribution
  • ‚Ä¢ The outlier IS the insight (fraud, errors to fix)

Remove/investigate if:

  • ‚Ä¢ It's likely a data entry error
  • ‚Ä¢ It's a test transaction
  • ‚Ä¢ It would distort your analysis
  • ‚Ä¢ You can verify it's invalid

üí° Best practice: Always document what you did with outliers and why. "Removed 12 orders over $10,000 as they were identified as wholesale bulk orders, not individual customer purchases."

Z-TEST / T-TEST Comparing 2 Groups ‚Äî Complete Mathematical Guide

üß† What's the Big Idea?

When you have two groups and want to know if they're truly different (not just randomly different), you're asking: "Could this difference have happened by pure chance?"

The Z-test and T-test answer this by calculating how "surprising" your observed difference is. If it's very surprising (unlikely to happen by chance), we say the difference is statistically significant.

‚öñÔ∏è Z-Test vs T-Test: Which One?

Use Z-Test when:

  • ‚Ä¢ Sample size is large (n > 30)
  • ‚Ä¢ You know the population standard deviation
  • ‚Ä¢ Comparing proportions (conversion rates)

Use T-Test when:

  • ‚Ä¢ Sample size is small (n ‚â§ 30)
  • ‚Ä¢ You DON'T know population std dev
  • ‚Ä¢ Comparing means (averages)

üìä PART A: Two-Sample Z-Test for Proportions (Conversion Rates)

Use this when comparing percentages between two groups, like "Did the new button increase click rate?"

üìã The Data You Need:

Symbol What It Means Example
$n_1$Sample size of Group 1 (Control)1,000 users saw old button
$n_2$Sample size of Group 2 (Treatment)1,000 users saw new button
$x_1$Number of "successes" in Group 130 clicked
$x_2$Number of "successes" in Group 245 clicked
$\hat{p}_1$Proportion for Group 1 = $x_1 / n_1$$30 / 1000 = 0.030$ (3.0%)
$\hat{p}_2$Proportion for Group 2 = $x_2 / n_2$$45 / 1000 = 0.045$ (4.5%)

üî¢ Step-by-Step Calculation:

Step 1: State Your Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis ($H_0$): "There is NO real difference. $p_1 = p_2$"

Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$): "There IS a real difference. $p_1 \neq p_2$"

Think of H‚ÇÄ as the "boring" hypothesis ‚Äî nothing interesting happened.

Step 2: Calculate the Pooled Proportion ($\hat{p}$)

This is the overall conversion rate if we combined both groups. We use this because under $H_0$, we assume both groups have the same true rate.

$$ \hat{p} = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{n_1 + n_2} $$
Example: pÃÇ = (30 + 45) √∑ (1000 + 1000) = 75 √∑ 2000 = 0.0375

Step 3: Calculate Standard Error (SE)

Standard Error measures how much random variation we'd expect in the difference between proportions. Bigger samples = smaller SE = more precise.

$$ SE = \sqrt{\hat{p}(1 - \hat{p}) \left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)} $$
Example ‚Äî Let's break it down:
  1. pÃÇ √ó (1 - pÃÇ) = 0.0375 √ó 0.9625 = 0.0361
  2. 1/n‚ÇÅ + 1/n‚ÇÇ = 1/1000 + 1/1000 = 0.002
  3. Multiply: 0.0361 √ó 0.002 = 0.0000722
  4. Square root: ‚àö0.0000722 = 0.0085

Step 4: Calculate the Z-Score

The Z-score tells you how many standard errors away from zero your observed difference is. Higher |Z| = more surprising = less likely due to chance.

$$ Z = \frac{\hat{p}_2 - \hat{p}_1}{SE} $$
Example:
  1. Difference: pÃÇ‚ÇÇ - pÃÇ‚ÇÅ = 0.045 - 0.030 = 0.015
  2. Z = 0.015 √∑ 0.0085 = 1.76

Step 5: Find the P-Value

The p-value answers: "If there's truly no difference (H‚ÇÄ is true), what's the probability of seeing a Z-score this extreme or more extreme?"

Common Z-scores and their two-tailed p-values:

|Z| = 1.65p ‚âà 0.10Not significant
|Z| = 1.96p ‚âà 0.05Borderline ‚ö†Ô∏è
|Z| = 2.58p ‚âà 0.01Significant ‚úì
|Z| = 3.29p ‚âà 0.001Highly significant ‚úì‚úì
Example: Our Z = 1.76, so p ‚âà 0.078. This is > 0.05, so NOT statistically significant.

Step 6: Make Your Decision

If p ‚â• 0.05:

Fail to reject H‚ÇÄ. The difference could be due to random chance. "Not enough evidence."

If p < 0.05:

Reject H‚ÇÄ. The difference is statistically significant. "Unlikely to be chance."

Our Example Conclusion: p = 0.078 > 0.05, so we cannot conclude the new button is better. The 1.5% difference might just be random noise.

üìä PART B: Two-Sample T-Test for Means (Averages)

Use this when comparing averages between two groups, like "Do premium users spend more than free users?"

üìã The Data You Need:

Symbol What It Means Example

üî¢ Step-by-Step Calculation:

$$ SE = \sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}} $$
Example:

ANOVA Comparing 3+ Groups ‚Äî Complete Mathematical Guide

üß† What's the Big Idea?

ANOVA stands for ANalysis Of VAriance. It compares the means of 3 or more groups simultaneously.

Why not just run multiple t-tests? If you compare Group A vs B, then B vs C, then A vs C, you inflate your chance of a false positive. With 3 comparisons at Œ±=0.05, your real error rate is ~14%, not 5%!

ANOVA controls this by asking one question: "Is there ANY difference among these groups?"

üí° The Core Concept: Comparing Two Types of Variance

Between-Group Variance (SSB)

How much do the group means differ from each other? If groups are truly different, this should be LARGE.

Within-Group Variance (SSW)

How much do individuals vary within their own groups? This is the "noise" or natural variation.

F = Between-Group Variance √∑ Within-Group Variance

If F is large ‚Üí Groups are more different from each other than expected by random noise

CORRELATION & REGRESSION Finding Relationships ‚Äî Complete Mathematical Guide

üß† What's the Big Idea?

Correlation measures the strength and direction of a relationship between two numeric variables. It answers: "When X goes up, does Y tend to go up (positive), down (negative), or show no pattern (zero)?"

Regression goes further ‚Äî it finds the best-fit line so you can predict Y from X. It answers: "By how much does Y change for each unit change in X?"

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Correlation ‚â† Causation

Example: Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are highly correlated. Does ice cream cause drowning? NO!

The truth: Both are caused by a third variable ‚Äî hot weather (a confounding variable).

Rule: Correlation shows association, not causation. Only controlled experiments can prove causation.

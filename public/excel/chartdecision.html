🧭 Chart Decision Tree

Choosing a chart isn't a matter of taste. Answer three questions — what am I trying to show, what kind of data do I have, and how many variables — and the right chart is usually already decided.

📚 Start here

A chart is a picture of your data. Our eyes extract patterns from pictures far faster than our minds extract them from tables — a good chart says in two seconds what a table takes two minutes to reveal. A bad chart takes just as long and can point the wrong way.

Using a pie chart for fifteen categories doesn't make the data harder to read by accident — it makes it genuinely unreadable, because nobody can compare fifteen angles. The bar chart of the same data is instantly clear. That's the whole game.

The three questions

🎯

1. What's the goal?

Compare · trend · composition · distribution · relationship · flow · location

📊

2. What data types?

Categorical · numeric · temporal · geographic

🔢

3. How many variables?

One · two · three or more

The four data types

📝 Categorical

Names and groups you can't do maths on.

Region, Category, Ship Mode, Segment

🔢 Numeric

Values you can add and average.

Sales, Profit, Quantity, Discount

📅 Temporal

Points or periods in time — they have a natural order.

Order Date, Ship Date, month, quarter

🗺️ Geographic

Places that can sit on a map.

State, City, Postal Code, Country

⚡ The 30-second answer

Nine times out of ten in business analysis, it's one of these five.

Bar chart
Comparing a number across categories. The default — and usually correct.
Line chart
Anything measured over time. Time always runs left to right.
Scatter plot
Does one number move with another? Discount vs Profit.
Stacked bar
Totals and what they're made of, side by side.
Histogram
How is one number spread out? Where do most values sit?
When you're not sure, start with a bar chart

It's the most accurately-read chart there is, because people judge lengths against a common baseline better than any other visual comparison. Only move to something else when you have a specific reason.

📈 Goal: change over time

Time on the horizontal axis, past on the left. Always.

Line chart Basic
One numeric value over many periods. Emphasises the shape of the trend.
Column chart Basic
Few periods, and you care about each period's total rather than the flow between them.
Sparkline Basic
A trend inside a single table cell. Perfect beside a KPI.
Area chart Basic
A line where the volume beneath matters. Must start at zero.
Slope chart Advanced
Exactly two points in time, many items. Who rose, who fell, by how much.
Stacked area Basic
Composition changing over time. Only the bottom band is easy to read.
Waterfall Uncommon
Decomposes the change between two totals into its contributing pieces.
Box plot series Basic
Many readings per period — shows how the whole spread evolves, not just the average.
Line or column?

Line emphasises flow and continuity — the story is the direction. Columns emphasise the magnitude of each individual period. Twelve months of sales: line. Four quarterly targets: columns.

⚖️ Goal: comparing groups

The most common task in business analysis. Make the differences unmissable.

Bar chart Basic
The gold standard. Horizontal when the labels are long — State and Product names.
Grouped bar Basic
Two categorical variables at once — Region × Segment. Keep it to 2–3 series.
Lollipop Uncommon
A bar chart on a diet. Much less ink when you have twenty-plus categories.
Dot plot Uncommon
Position instead of length — the right choice when zero isn't a meaningful baseline.
Dumbbell Uncommon
Two values per category — this year vs last, actual vs target. The gap is the message.
Bump chart Uncommon
Tracks rank over time rather than value. Who overtook whom.
Violin plot Uncommon
Compares whole distributions across groups, not just their averages.
Small multiples Basic
One small chart per group on a shared scale. The cure for an overcrowded chart.
Sort your bars

Unless the categories have a natural order (months, sizes, ratings), sort by value. An alphabetical bar chart forces the reader to do the ranking that the chart should have done for them.

🥧 Goal: part-to-whole

How a total breaks down. Be honest about how hard these are to read precisely.

Pie chart Basic
Only for 2–5 slices with clearly different sizes. Angles are hard to compare — use sparingly.
Donut Uncommon
A pie with a hole — useful because you can put the total in the middle.
100% stacked bar Basic
Usually better than a pie. People judge lengths far more accurately than angles.
Treemap Advanced
Nested proportions — Category → Sub-Category. Good for many parts, poor for precision.
Waffle / grid Uncommon
A 10×10 grid of squares. Countable, so percentages read exactly.
Mosaic / Marimekko Advanced
Both axes carry proportion — segment share and mix within each segment.
The pie chart test

Before using one, ask: could a reader rank the slices correctly without the labels? With three clearly different slices, yes. With eight similar ones, no — and at that point the chart is decoration, not communication. Use a sorted bar chart instead.

📊 Goal: distribution

Where do the values sit, how spread out are they, and are there outliers? This is the exploratory step people skip — and then get surprised later.

Histogram Basic
One numeric variable, binned into ranges. Start here, always.
Box plot Basic
Median, middle 50%, whiskers and outliers. Compact enough to repeat per group.
Density curve Uncommon
A smoothed histogram. Better for overlaying two distributions.
Strip / swarm plot Uncommon
Every individual point. Reveals clusters and gaps a histogram's bins can hide.
Ridgeline Advanced
Stacked density curves — how a distribution shifts across many groups or periods.
Count bar chart Basic
The distribution of a categorical variable — how many orders per Ship Mode.
Why this matters more than it looks

Averages lie about skewed data. Superstore's average sale is about $230, but the median is around $54 — a handful of enormous orders drag the mean upward. A histogram shows you that in one glance, and stops you reporting a "typical" order size that almost nobody actually placed.

🔗 Goal: relationships

Does one variable move with another?

Scatter plot Basic
Two numeric variables. The workhorse for spotting correlation.
Bubble chart Basic
A scatter plot with size as a third variable. Three is the limit — resist a fourth.
Heatmap Basic
A grid where colour encodes the value. Region × Category at a glance.
Combo chart Uncommon
Two metrics on different scales — Sales as bars, margin % as a line. Label both axes clearly.
Connected scatter Uncommon
Two metrics evolving together, joined in time order. Shows trajectories and loops.
Network diagram Advanced
Entities and the connections between them. Needs specialist tooling.
Correlation is not causation — and a scatter plot won't tell you which

In the Superstore data, higher discounts correlate strongly with lower profit. That's a real pattern worth acting on, but the chart alone can't tell you whether discounting causes the losses or whether unprofitable products simply get discounted more often. Charts raise questions; they don't settle them.

🔄 Goal: flows and processes

Movement through stages, and where volume is lost along the way.

Funnel Basic
Sequential stages with drop-off — visitors → carts → orders.
Sankey Advanced
Band width is volume. Handles splits and merges across several stages.
Gantt Basic
Tasks against a timeline — start, end, duration and overlap.
Parallel sets Advanced
How groups split across several categorical dimensions in sequence.

🗺️ Goal: geography

Choropleth Basic
Regions shaded by value. Use rates, not raw counts — otherwise it's a population map.
Bubble map Basic
Size at a location. Handles raw totals honestly, unlike a choropleth.
Scatter map Basic
Individual points at coordinates. Good until the points start overlapping.
Connection map Advanced
Movement between places — shipments, routes, migration.
Maps are seductive and often the wrong choice

A map answers "where", not "how much". If your real question is which states sell the most, a sorted bar chart is faster to read and far more precise — geography only earns its space when spatial position is part of the insight (clusters, distance, adjacency).

🔢 Goal: a single number

Sometimes the most effective visualisation is no chart at all.

KPI card Basic
One critical number, big. No comparison needed.
KPI + indicator Basic
The number plus its direction of travel. Almost always better than the number alone.
Bullet chart Uncommon
Actual vs target with context bands. Everything a gauge does, in a fifth of the space.
Table Basic
When readers need exact values, or to look up specific rows. Add data bars for a hint of shape.

⚠️ Charts to avoid

Not because they're ugly — because they systematically mislead.

Radar / spider Avoid
The perceived area changes if you reorder the axes — and the order is always arbitrary. Use instead: a grouped bar chart or parallel coordinates.
Circular bar Avoid
Outer rings travel further for the same value, so equal numbers look unequal. Use instead: a plain bar chart.
Pictogram Avoid
Half an icon isn't half a value to the eye, and rounding to whole icons loses precision. Use instead: a bar chart, or a waffle if you want countability.
3D anything Avoid
Perspective makes near bars look larger than far ones at identical values. Use instead: the same chart, flat.
And one habit, not a chart type

Truncating the y-axis on a bar chart. Bars encode value by length, so cutting off the baseline makes a 2% difference look like a 200% one. Line charts may start elsewhere when the variation is small and you say so — bar charts must start at zero, always.

🎚️ The encoding ladder

Some visual channels are read more accurately than others. This ranking, from decades of perception research, explains almost every recommendation on this page: put your most important number in the most accurate channel available.

RankChannelExampleAccuracy
1Position on a common scaleScatter plot, dot plotBest
2Length from a shared baselineBar chartExcellent
3Slope / angleLine chartGood
4AreaBubble chart, treemapFair
5Colour intensityHeatmap, choroplethRough
6Colour hueCategorical coloursIdentifies, doesn't quantify
7Volume / 3D3D chartsWorst — avoid
This is why the bar chart keeps winning

It uses channel 2 for the number that matters. A pie chart uses angle and area; a heatmap uses colour intensity. Those aren't wrong — they're just less precise, so reserve them for when you're showing pattern rather than magnitude.

✅ Final refinement checklist

Run every finished chart past these. Most improvements come from removing, not adding.

  • One chart, one message. If you can't say what it shows in a sentence, split it.
  • The title states the finding, not the fields. "West leads on sales but trails on margin" beats "Sales by Region".
  • Sorted meaningfully. By value, unless the categories have a natural order.
  • Bar charts start at zero. Non-negotiable.
  • Axes are labelled with units. Is that dollars, thousands, or percent?
  • Colour carries meaning. If every bar is a different colour for decoration, make them all one colour and highlight only the bar you're talking about.
  • Colour-blind safe. Roughly 1 in 12 men can't separate red from green — don't let that pair be the only signal.
  • Legend removed where possible. One series needs no legend; direct labels beat a legend every time.
  • Gridlines faded or gone. They should support the data, not compete with it.
  • Crowded? Use small multiples. Six small charts on one scale beat one chart with six series.
  • Long labels? Go horizontal. Rotated text is a reading tax.
  • The five-second test. Show it to someone uninvolved. If they don't get the point in five seconds, simplify — the problem is the chart, not them.
